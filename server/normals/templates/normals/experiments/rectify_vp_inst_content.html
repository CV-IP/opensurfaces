Step 1

Drag the thumbtack so that it appears to lie flush against the surface in an image. The surface is outlined with a red shape. When you are done, the red thumbtack pin should appear to face away from the surface.

We suggest aligning the two black lines so they are parallel with other lines in the photograph. Drag with the right mouse button to rotate the black lines. This will make step 2 easier.

Step 2

On the right, you will see a distorted version of the shape. This is what the shape looks like if you are viewing it from a top-down view. If the thumbtack is correct, then this distorted shape should appear to face forward.

To adjust the shape on the right, you can drag it with the mouse. Please also rotate it with the right mouse button so that it is upright.

Please rotate the shape by dragging with the right mouse button so that any patterns in the shape are aligned vertically. Adjusting the orientation should also become easier when the shape is aligned.

For example, if there is a tile pattern in the shape, the tiles should be parallel in the right image. If it is a square in the scene, it should appear as a square in the right image. The outline of the shape on the right is not important; the texture and patterns inside is what matters.

Adjusting the result

If dragging moves the shape too fast, click the "Fine adjustment" button. You may also zoom with the "Zoom in" button.

Some surfaces will not have a repeating texture. Do your best to align it so that the thumbtack faces away from the surface and the right shape faces forward.

In some scenes, the thumbtack may no longer look correct once you get the right side aligned properly. This is fine; it is more important that the shape on the right looks good. Patterns inside the shape should appear parallel and not slanted.

The frame has 90 degree angles and faces forward.

Why are we doing this?

We are trying to understand the 3D structure of photographs. These thumbtack orientations allow us to rectify shapes so that they appear to face the camera. This experiment is part of ongoing research at Cornell University. Thanks for participating!
